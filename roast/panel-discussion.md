# Panel Discussion — CulturalGeeks Site Review
**Format:** Roundtable synthesis after independent reviews
**Participants:** Shreya Iyer (Brand), Marcus Tan (Design), Ankit Verma (Growth), Meera Nambiar (Customer), Rohit Kapoor (Investor)

---

## Opening Round: What Do We Agree On?

**Shreya:** The writing is the site's greatest asset and also its biggest risk. The hero paragraph is genuinely good — the kind of practitioner-voice copy that is nearly impossible to fake. But that quality of writing sets an expectation the rest of the page does not always meet. The testimonials, in particular, break the spell.

**Meera:** Agreed. I stayed on the page because of the writing. I nearly left because of the testimonials.

**Marcus:** From a design perspective, the aesthetic is coherent and the right choice for the audience. My concerns are functional: the form, the mobile nav, the chatbot accessibility. These are not design-philosophy problems — they are implementation gaps that a few days of work could close.

**Ankit:** My concerns are more structural. The single biggest issue is that the contact form does not work. Everything else we are discussing is secondary to that. If someone submits an enquiry today, it goes nowhere. That is a revenue failure, not a design failure.

**Rohit:** I want to reinforce Ankit's point. The form is P0. But I would add: the business architecture problem is more strategic. The site does not communicate what stage this business is at, what traction it has, or how the four services relate to each other as a portfolio. That is not just a copy problem — it reflects a positioning decision that has not yet been fully made.

---

## The Central Tension: Is This a Personal Brand or a Business?

**Shreya:** This is the most important unresolved question on the site, and it runs through everything. "CulturalGeeks" sounds like a platform — something bigger than one person. But the entire site is written in the first person: "I have negotiated in Shanghai," "I provide the intelligence," "I design and deliver workshops." There is no team, no associate consultant, no partner. It is a solo practice dressed in platform clothing.

**Rohit:** That is not necessarily a problem — many of the most successful knowledge businesses are founder-led. But if it is a founder-led practice, own it. Put Ehtesham's face on the site. Use his name prominently. The authority is personal.

**Meera:** From my perspective as a potential client, I would actually prefer the personal signal. I am not hiring "CulturalGeeks" as an institution. I am hiring this specific person with this specific experience. The brand name slightly obscures that. If the homepage said "Mohammad Ehteshamul Haque, known as Ehtesham — intercultural business consultant and author" in the first sentence, I would trust it more immediately.

**Ankit:** The SEO implication of this is real too. "CulturalGeeks" has no search equity. Ehtesham's name (or a derivatives of it) plus relevant keywords — "India market entry consultant," "intercultural business communication" — is where the search demand actually lives.

---

## Debate: Should "CulturalGeeks" Stay?

**Shreya:** I raised this in my review. The name has a tension with the premium positioning. "Geeks" signals enthusiasm and technical obsession — which is fine for a product company, but carries risk for an advisor being introduced to a VP at a German manufacturing firm.

**Marcus:** The logo handles the name well — the typography makes "GEEKS" feel editorial rather than casual. But the logo can only do so much. In a text context — an email subject line, a LinkedIn headline, a Google search result — "CulturalGeeks" does not carry the same weight.

**Rohit:** I would not necessarily change it, but I would be aware of the contexts in which it creates friction. The consultancy brand might be "CulturalGeeks" while Ehtesham's personal authority brand leads with his name and credentials. Many author-consultants operate this way — the platform name and the personal name work together.

**Meera:** Practically speaking: when I tell a colleague about this site, I say "there's this consultant, Ehtesham..." The brand name comes second. Build the site around the person first, the brand second.

---

## Unanimous Consensus Points

After reviewing all five perspectives, the panel finds unanimous agreement on the following:

### 1. The Contact Form Must Be Fixed Before Launch
Every panellist flagged this independently. The fake `setTimeout` submission is the most immediately damaging issue on the site. All enquiries are currently lost. This is a P0.

### 2. A Headshot Is Non-Negotiable
Four out of five panellists flagged the absence of a photograph. For a personal advisory brand at the senior executive level, the person *is* the product. The compass and the book cover are beautiful but they do not replace a face.

### 3. The Testimonials Must Change
All five panellists noted the testimonials. The choice is: get real testimonials with real names (preferred), or remove the section until you have them. Keeping anonymous placeholder-style quotes damages the credibility the rest of the site has built.

### 4. The Book Status Needs Clarity
"Get the Book" → contact form is confusing and erodes trust. If the book is not yet published, say so clearly and offer a pre-order or notification mechanism.

### 5. The Hero Copy Is the Site's Strongest Asset
Preserve the "wrong container" story. Do not let future edits water it down in pursuit of brevity or keyword optimisation. It is doing more work than any other element on the page.

## Areas of Disagreement

**Should pricing or engagement format be included?**
- Ankit (Growth): Yes — even "typically a 3-month engagement" gives buyers enough to self-qualify
- Rohit (Investor): Agree — reduces friction for the right buyer
- Shreya (Brand): Hesitant — price anchoring too early can filter out buyers who would value it more if they understood the scope first
- Meera (Customer): I want format information (half-day workshop? 6-month retainer?) more than I want pricing
- Marcus (Design): No opinion on business model; would implement whatever is decided

**Resolution:** Add engagement format signals (duration, team size, format type) without hard prices. Leave consultation call as the pricing gateway.

**Should the brand name change?**
- Shreya (Brand): Warrants a serious discussion; name creates friction in premium contexts
- Rohit (Investor): Can coexist with a strong personal brand; lower priority
- Meera (Customer): Doesn't bother me personally; Ehtesham's name matters more
- Ankit (Growth): SEO case for personal name is strong; brand name change is a longer-term consideration
- Marcus (Design): The logo handles it well; name change is out of scope for this build

**Resolution:** No immediate change. But ensure Ehtesham's full name and a clear position statement ("intercultural business consultant, author") appear prominently on the homepage. The brand name can coexist once the personal authority is established.

---

## What Needs to Happen, In What Order

1. **Fix the form** (all leads currently lost — fix before launch, not after)
2. **Add a photograph** (personal brand without a face is a contradiction)
3. **Replace testimonials** (real names or remove entirely)
4. **Clarify book status** (unpublished book marketed as available damages trust)
5. **Add Open Graph tags** (LinkedIn sharing is a primary channel; broken previews kill sharing)
6. **Add engagement format signals to services** (removes barrier for self-qualified buyers)
7. **Add email capture below contact form** (captures mid-funnel visitors who are not ready to book)
8. **Wire up analytics** (GA4 takes 10 minutes; without it there is no visibility)

Items 1–4 are pre-launch P0. Items 5–8 are post-launch P1.

---

## Closing Statement

**Shreya:** This is a site that is 70% of the way to being genuinely excellent. The aesthetic, the copy voice, the credential architecture, the conceptual framework — these are the hard parts, and they are largely done. The gaps are operational and tactical, not strategic. Fix the plumbing first. Then refine.

**Rohit:** What I would tell Ehtesham: you have built an impressive brochure. The next version needs to be an engine. Add traction signals, close the lead capture loops, and make the book a real entry point into a structured journey. That is when this becomes a business, not just a website.
